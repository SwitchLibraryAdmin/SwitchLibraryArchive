Section 01

// Classified Research — EU Commission, 2017

The €360,000 Secret

In 2017, the European Commission spent nearly half a million euros on a landmark study surveying 30,000 consumers across 6 EU countries (UK, Germany, France, Spain, Poland, Sweden). The findings were so inconvenient, the report was quietly shelved—until it was leaked.

// Key Finding

“Unlike movies or books, unauthorized access to video games statistically increased legal sales.”

The study highlighted a systemic lack of legal demos, aggressive region-locking, and prohibitive pricing as the primary drivers of unauthorized downloads.

Context: The EU data suggests that "piracy" is often a market correction—a symptom of consumers bypassing walled gardens to trial software or access geo-restricted content before making a legitimate purchase. Read the full study (PDF) →

Section 02

Piracy Is a Service Problem

// Gabe Newell — Co-founder & CEO, Valve Corporation

“Piracy is almost always a service problem and not a pricing problem.”

Valve went on to build Steam — the platform that effectively ended PC game piracy in every market it entered with competitive, localized pricing.

// DRM: The Promise

  • Prevents unauthorized copying and protects revenue

  • Acts as a meaningful deterrent against casual piracy

  • Ensures only paying customers can access the product

// DRM: The Reality

  • Every major DRM in history has been cracked — most within days of launch. Denuvo. SecuROM. Nintendo’s own encryption. All of them.

  • The paying customer bears 100% of the friction: always-online checks, install limits, account requirements, server shutdowns.

  • The unauthorized version is frequently faster, cleaner, and works offline. DRM often artificially degrades the product for the paying consumer.

The Steam Effect: When Valve launched in Russia and Eastern Europe with regionally adjusted pricing, legitimate purchases in markets previously synonymous with rampant piracy surged dramatically. No new laws. No lawsuits. Just a service that was faster, more convenient, and cheaper than pirating. Newell’s conclusion wasn’t philosophical — it was empirical.

Section 04

The Walled Garden & § 1201

// Key Finding

Your iPhone: legally jailbreakable. Your tractor: legally moddable. Your Switch: federal offense.

Under 17 U.S.C. § 1201, the Library of Congress has granted exemptions for smartphones and smart TVs — but not game consoles.

The Right to Repair

17 U.S.C. § 1201

The DMCA makes it illegal to bypass digital locks on copyrighted works. However, the Library of Congress routinely grants exemptions to this rule when it harms consumers. They have legally protected the jailbreaking of smartphones, the modification of smart TVs, and the right of farmers to bypass DRM to repair their own tractors.

Yet, video game consoles remain aggressively guarded. When hardware manufacturers use software locks to prevent unauthorized repairs, third-party operating systems, or custom modifications, it acts as an artificial walled garden, preventing fair competition.

Section 05

A Developer’s Perspective

Game developer Lachhh (creator of Just Shapes & Beats) took a radically different approach to unverified access—proving that ethical engagement is more effective than aggressive DRM.

// Developer Statement

“I’d rather you play the game and not pay, than not play at all.”

— Lachhh, Just Shapes & Beats in-game unauthorized copy screen

just_shapes_and_beats_unauthorized_access.mp4

Just Shapes & Beats — Unauthorized Copy Screen

Instead of DRM, lawsuits, or guilt-tripping, Lachhh built a custom screen into the unauthorized version of his game. It plays a short message thanking the player, asks them to consider buying the game if they enjoy it, and then lets them keep playing with no restrictions. The approach went viral and drove a measurable spike in sales.
