Is My Switch Hackable?

The short answer: Yes, but the method is much easier if your switch was made before June 2018

Only the earliest "V1" Nintendo Switch consoles are vulnerable to a software-based exploit, meaning they can be hacked using only a computer and a simple USB cable. These unpatched units were manufactured between the console's launch in March 2017 and roughly July 2018.

If your Switch was manufactured after the summer of 2018—including patched V1s, and all V2s, Lites, and OLED models—Nintendo physically fixed the hardware vulnerability, so the only way to hack these newer consoles is by micro-soldering a physical modchip directly to the motherboard.

Is My Switch Unpatched?

Because the manufacturing transition happened in the middle of 2018, the only foolproof way to know if your Switch is an unpatched V1 is to check its serial number. You can find this on the white sticker on the bottom edge of your console.

To verify your hardware, enter your serial number prefix at ismyswitchpatched.com, or check this chart:

What items are Required?
An Unpatched V1 Switch

Verify using the tool linked above. If your console is "Potentially Patched," there is a high likelihood it is patched. The only way to know for sure is to attempt injecting a payload. See below.

MicroSD Card (256GB+ Recommended, 64GB Minimum)

I highly recommend a high-quality card to ensure fast boot and load and download times.

An RCM Jig

This small piece of plastic and wire safely shorts the pins in your right Joy-Con rail to boot the console into ReCovery Mode. Highly recommended over using tinfoil or paperclips, which can permanently damage your console pins.

A Payload Sender & Data Cable

You need a device to send the exploit code into the Switch. A PC is recommended, though an Android phone or a jailbroken iOS device can also work. You will also need a high-quality USB-C cable capable of data transfer (not just charging) to connect the two devices.

What if My Console is Patched?
This guide only covers unpatched and already modded Switches. The only way to hack a Patched V1, V2, Lite, or OLED is by soldering a modchip (like the HWFLY or Picofly) to the motherboard. This is not hard if you have experience doing fine work, but certainly not a first time soldering job. Nintendo has cracked down hard on production, so these chips are usually sourced through third-party Chinese distributors.
What if my Switch is "Potentially Patched?"

If your serial number is "Potentially Patched," the only way to know the truth is to physically test the hardware by attempting to inject a payload. This process requires no MicroSD card and makes zero permanent modifications to your console.

1

Download TegraRcmGUI & biskeydump.bin

Download and install TegraRcmGUI on your Windows PC. Then download the biskeydump.bin payload. When you first connect your Switch in RCM mode, TegraRcmGUI will prompt you to install a driver — allow it.

If automatic driver installation fails, download Zadig. Your Switch will appear as "APX" or "Unknown Device." Select it, choose the libusbK driver, and click Install Driver.

2

Enter RCM Mode

Boot your Switch into ReCovery Mode using your RCM jig. Learn how to enter RCM here →

3

Connect, Inject & Read the Output

Plug your Switch into your PC with a USB-C data cable, load biskeydump.bin in TegraRcmGUI, and click Inject. Do not look at the Switch screen — look at the text log output in the TegraRcmGUI window.

0x7000

Your Switch is unpatched. You're good to go — continue to the guide below.

0x0000

Your Switch is patched. Software exploits won't work. A modchip is the only option.
